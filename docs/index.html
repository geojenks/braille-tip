    What you're building

    Braille-tip is a compact (11 mm Ø hex) soft tactile sensor that mounts on a pen and reads Braille as you sweep it left-to-right. A soft silicone membrane carries 19 taxels (1 mm Ø, on a 2.5 mm grid matching Braille dot spacing). Each taxel is hydraulically coupled down a fluid channel; pressing a taxel displaces fluid, read out either optically (dyed water + camera) or capacitively (liquid metal + an outer conductive plate — the Coil-Tac mechanism).

    The forks (two example flows, side by side)

    The columns are just two sensible recipes — not a fixed pairing. Any combination works; only Fork 1 (transduction) cascades, setting the liquid, readout and maintenance.

    Key fact: the camera never images the silicone tip — it films a downstream length of transparent tubing. Whether the tip is clear or opaque black makes no difference to either transduction.

    Tools & materials

    Common to all builds

    • Host felt-tip pen (felt + tip removed; barrel carries the tubing). You can also print the pen shaft yourself — handy for concealing electronics or even a camera (as in the left-hand unit above).
    • Something to read: embossed Braille card works, but isn't required — better, 3D-print your own Braille characters so you can lay out arbitrary letters and words for testing.
    • Fine syringes + blunt needles; hobby knife; tweezers.

    Printers — either printer works for either build

    • FDM — project used a Bambu Lab, but any works. Makes casting easier; preferred for larger builds. No UV/IPA post-processing.
    • Resin/SLA — project used a Formlabs Form 3. Much more precise; needs UV cure + IPA wash post-processing.

    Fork 2ACast silicone tip

    • Ecoflex 00-30 (Smooth-On) platinum-cure silicone — a good middle ground.
    • Note: Ecoflex 00-10 cures tacky; you can fix it with a thin film of another silicone, but it's fiddly. 00-30 avoids the bother.
    • 4-part printed mould — CAD in the braille-tip repo (Fabrication/).

    Fork 2BDirect-print silicone tip

    • Silicone-capable printer (Form 3); tip comes out opaque black.
    • Clear-resin backing that plugs into the printed tip (Step 6).

    Fork 1AOptical transduction

    • Black food dye (Sainsbury's) + water.
    • Thin transparent tubing. Glass capillaries (0.6 mm ID) aren't strictly necessary — but the thinner the tubing, the stronger the signal (1→0.6 mm narrowing amplifies meniscus travel ×2.78).
    • A camera + edge-finding CV. A smartphone camera is almost certainly good enough.

    Fork 1BCapacitive transduction (Coil-Tac)

    • Liquid metal Ga:In:Sn 69:22:9 (EGaIn) — e.g. Smart-elements GmbH.
    • Insulating channel for the LM — PTFE or silicone tubing. Diameter is arbitrary (it scales with your design); here it's 1 mm ID / 2 mm OD.
    • Outer capacitive plate (senses the moving liquid metal): any conductor. More LM works, but avoid LM where possible — it's corrosive, so a copper tube that slips around the insulated LM channel is ideal.
    • NaOH cap: caustic soda + water (anti-oxidation; consumed quickly).
    • Capacitance readout — either an LCR meter (more accurate), or a voltage reader / microcontroller (project used an Arduino Due, 3.3 V, + a reference capacitor ~15 pF, capacitors-in-series). The microcontroller is a nice substitute because it can read many channels in parallel, but it's less accurate.
    • Grounding the LM: a metal push-fit coupling (only if the build is big enough), otherwise a corrosion-resistant tungsten wire that dips into the liquid metal.
    2

    Print the parts — Fork 3: FDM vs Resin

    Either printer works for either build:

    • FDM Bambu Lab / any — cheaper, makes casting easier → preferred for larger builds. No UV/IPA post-processing.
    • Resin/SLA Form 3 / any — much more precise → preferred for small builds.

    Resin post-processing (resin only)

    Every resin print gets a UV cure + IPA wash. If silicone will later cure against the part, this must be VERY thorough — uncured resin inhibits platinum-cure silicone. Repeat cure/wash cycles (~5× in the paper) until silicone reliably sets against it.

    Assembly overview

    3

    Make the tactile tip — Fork 2

    Fork 2ACast the silicone tip in a mould

    1. Mix Ecoflex 00-30 per datasheet; degas if you can.
    2. Pour into the (decontaminated, if resin) mould.
    3. Place the sensor base on top and extrude the silicone out of the taxel cavities.
    4. Cure at ~40–50 °C (don't use a normal oven — unreliable temperature will melt the plastic parts); demould.

    Fork 2BDirect-print the silicone tip

    1. Print the tip directly (opaque black silicone).
    2. Print the clear-resin backing that plugs into it (Step 6).
    Trade-off: direct printing skips moulding and is fast/repeatable at small scale. Tip opacity does not affect transduction (the camera films downstream tubing, not the tip). But you can't see inside an opaque tip, so some steps — e.g. filling the cavities — become a bit of a shoot-and-hope.
    4

    Channels & tubing — Fork 4

    How the taxels connect to their fluid channels is a trade between design effort and fabrication effort — and it's independent of how you made the tip (print the tip and use normal tubing, or cast the tip and print a channel matrix — any combination works).

    Fork 4ACoupled tubing — low design load, higher fabrication load

    Fit existing tubing to the sensor. Two methods:

    • Printed barbs — slide elastomeric tubing onto each barbed shaft; ensure enough spacing between barbs for the tubing. Fiddly at 19-channel density.
    • Threaded insert + push-fit coupler — heat-set a threaded insert into a slightly-undersized hole with a soldering iron (e.g. RS threaded inserts), then screw in a threaded push-fit coupler. Roomy/expensive — best when you have the space (e.g. LM builds).

    Fork 4BPrinted channel matrix — high design load, low fabrication load

    CAD all the channels into a (clear) resin part so they're integral to the sensor — there's nothing to couple; you fill the channels directly (Step 5). This is exactly the clear resin backing used in the merged device (Step 6). For capacitance it's proven; for the optical readout this "printed tubing" route is unexplored but promising.

    5

    Fill with working fluid — Fork 5

    Air bubbles are the main failure mode for both fluids — work slowly and tap them out.
    • 5A Back-fill then top up: introduce liquid "behind" the tubing at the interface, fill the tubes most of the way, then attach.
    • 5B Direct syringe: if the assembly is small/accessible enough, inject directly with a fine syringe.
    6

    Merged device assembly (the Coil-Tac Braille-tip)

    The proven merge: it looks like the original Braille-tip but uses liquid metal.

    1. Direct-print the tip in opaque black silicone (Fork 2B).
    2. Print a clear resin backing — this is the printed channel matrix (Fork 4B), decontaminated (Step 2), that plugs directly into the tip.
    3. Per channel, place the outer capacitive plate — a copper tube that slips around the insulated LM channel (a coil if the channel must flex). (This is the novel step, shown below.)
    4. Back-fill the liquid metal from behind the channel, then seal it off; ground it with tungsten wire (or a push-fit coupler if large enough).
    5. Read out per Step 7B.
    ⚠️ NaOH is consumed fast: the ions in the NaOH cap are used up quickly. Mitigations: work fast, evacuate the air and replace with argon, or use another liquid conductor (NaOH itself still works reasonably well).
    Status note: connections are very fiddly at this scale. The merge was demonstrated over a few taxels, not all 19 at once — completing the full-array integration is the obvious next step.
    7

    Read-out & signal processing — Fork 1

    Fork 1AOptical (dyed water + camera)

    Hardware. A camera images a downstream length of transparent tubing (not the tip). I used glass capillaries to keep it thin and inflexible; transparent tubing will still work.

    Software (separate, and ripe for improvement). The current pipeline normalises each channel 0→1, thresholds to binarise dot/no-dot (I used 0.4 with smoothing decay 4 — my specific settings), then segments letters + dictionary-looks-up → text/audio. This software is very inefficient and should be changed / improved upon.

    Hand-off recommendation: don't hand-write the CV. Record a video first, have an LLM scaffold the edge-finding + thresholding from the recording; go live/real-time only if needed. Treat the GitHub MATLAB as a reference, not a dependency.

    Fork 1BCapacitive (Coil-Tac)

    • Measure capacitance with an LCR meter (most accurate), or via capacitors-in-series on a voltage reader / microcontroller (project: Arduino Due, 3.3 V, reference cap ~15 pF — expect to tune). The microcontroller route trades accuracy for reading many channels in parallel.
    • Liquid-metal column length ↔ capacitance; calibrate against known fixed capacitors.
    • Scale per-channel by adding outer plates + reader channels; mechanism is low-power.
    8

    Maintenance & known failure modes

    Fork 1ADyed water upkeep

    • Black food dye (Sainsbury's) lasts ~days to a week, then goes gummy → flush and refill.
    • Worth designing a flush system so refreshing is fast.

    Fork 1BLiquid metal upkeep

    • Needs the NaOH cap; it's consumed quickly and must be replenished (see Step 6 mitigations: argon, work fast, alternative conductor).
    • Oxide skin changes LM behaviour — keep it capped and submerged (as in Step 6); calibrate with a small LM overhang into the static section to null the NaOH/fringing contribution.

    General

    • Bubbles in channels = bad signal — re-fill (Step 5).
    • The copper outer plate must hold its geometry; if it bends, switch to a coil.
    9

    Next steps for whoever picks this up

    • Work with scaled-up Braille letters first. Scaling down is a manufacturing/building challenge that is easy to solve with better equipment and isn't the interesting part. Miniaturising is theoretically straightforward, practically frustrating, and not interesting — though it is the final step to making the device usable.
    • A printable Braille test set — a library of independent Braille characters that snap into arbitrary words and lines for systematic testing, rather than relying on embossed card.
    • Integrate the liquid-metal readout across all 19 taxels (only a few proven so far).
    • Improved (ML?) algorithm for bump recognition.
    • Battery-powered, pocket-sized build.
    • Orientation-robust algorithm (don't require a straight perpendicular sweep).
    • Roller/guide to keep the tip parallel to the page; short user studies with Braille learners.
    A

    Appendix — key numbers

    • Tip: 11 mm hex OD; 19 taxels, 1 mm Ø, 2.5 mm centres.
    • Optical narrowing 1→0.6 mm ID → ×2.78 meniscus amplification; dot raises membrane ~0.48 mm.
    • Optical (my settings): smoothing decay 4; normalise 0→1; threshold 0.4; ~84.8% hand accuracy.
    • Liquid metal: Ga:In:Sn 69:22:9; PTFE 1 mm ID / 2 mm OD; copper outer plate; coil pitch ~1.5 mm.
    • Readout: Arduino Due 3.3 V, ~15 pF reference; ε_r ≈ 2.4.
    • Coil-Tac demo (7-ch dome): contact location RMSE 0.23 mm.
